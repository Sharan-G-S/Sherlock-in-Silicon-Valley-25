🕵️‍♂️ The Breach

The Silicon Valley Cybercrime Division (SVCD) has been hit with the most sophisticated security breach in history. A classified AI project—codenamed “Project Sentinel”—was compromised, with highly confidential AI security blueprints stolen. This breach is no ordinary cybercrime; it was an inside job, executed with military precision, leaving behind no traditional digital footprints.

However, the hackers made one mistake—they underestimated the power of AI forensics and the capabilities of a next-generation detective.

Desperate for answers, the SVCD calls upon you, the modern-day Sherlock, to crack the case before it’s too late.

Your mission? Unmask the 4-6 cybercriminals behind this heist, uncover their hidden connections, and bring them to justice before they wipe their tracks forever.

🕵️‍♂️ The Investigation

Your investigation will be divided into three intense rounds, each revealing key pieces of the puzzle.

🕵️‍♂️ ROUND 1: The Anomaly Detection (Unmasking Suspicious Activity)

The cybercriminals accessed SVCD’s secure database using multiple fake identities, manipulated login credentials, and unusual access patterns. Your task is to analyze the security logs and detect suspicious anomalies in:

  • User IDs: Repeated or fake IDs.
  • Login Status: Frequent logins from different locations.
  • IP Addresses: Unusual geographic patterns.
  • Login Times: Accessing restricted files at odd hours.
  • Activity Logs: Unusual actions performed inside the system.

⚠️ Data Set: A total of 250 login records, including repeated fraudulent users trying to cover their tracks.

🎯 Objective:

  • Detect the anomalies and shortlist potential suspects.
  • Identify the main orchestrators of the breach based on login patterns.

📜 ROUND 2: The Encrypted Clue Hunt (Extracting Hidden Evidence)

A hidden folder was discovered, containing 30 encrypted images—all left behind by the hackers. The cops couldn’t decode them, but you, Sherlock, have access to advanced AI-powered Optical Character Recognition (OCR) tools.

The hidden messages inside these images hold vital details about the hackers’ next move. But be warned—some clues are planted to mislead you.

🔹 Clue Type: Handwritten notes, screenshots of chat logs, encoded alphanumeric patterns.

🔹 Decryption Challenge: Use AI-powered OCR to extract text from images.

🔹 Cyber Investigation Twist: Some images are decoys—designed to waste time or mislead detectives.

🎯 Objective:

  • Extract and analyze the hidden text.
  • Cross-reference suspicious communication with the data from Round 1.
  • Find connections between the suspects—who is working together?

📊 ROUND 3: Sentiment Analysis of Hacker Communications

After identifying a pattern of communication from the extracted clues, you now have access to an intercepted set of hacker messages—but they are coded in casual conversations to hide their true intent.

Using AI-driven sentiment analysis, you must determine:

  • Who is the mastermind?
  • Who are the accomplices?
  • Who is just a decoy?

💡 The Catch: The hackers don’t directly mention the crime—they use coded language like:

  • “The shipment is ready” (Referring to the stolen data being transferred).
  • “We need more backup” (Recruiting more insiders).
  • “This is too risky, I’m out” (A member getting scared and wanting to leave).

⚠️ Data Set: 200 intercepted messages from various chat platforms, emails, and forums.

🎯 Objective:

  • Decode hidden meanings behind conversations.
  • Find connections between hackers (Who communicated with whom?).
  • Confirm the final 4-6 culprits and prepare a final report to convict them.

🕵️‍♂️ The Final Report

Once all three rounds are completed, participants must present their Final Case Report containing:

  • The names of all 4-6 culprits.
  • The mastermind behind the heist.
  • Evidence proving their involvement (based on rounds 1, 2, and 3).
  • How they executed the crime and their motives.

💥 Winning Criteria:

  • The first detective (or team) to correctly identify all culprits wins the game.
  • If some suspects remain unidentified, the case is considered partially solved.
  • If the wrong individuals are accused, the case remains unsolved, and the hackers escape.
